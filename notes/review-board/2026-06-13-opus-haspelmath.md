# Review: "Kinds as Projectibility Profiles"

**Seat:** Martin Haspelmath — Typology End-User (standpoint seat, based on public work on comparative concepts and measurement design; not a claim about the real person's views)

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

A kind is a worldly property-and-relation pattern whose partial observation licenses specified inferences under specified conditions (a "projectibility profile"), and "homeostatic" should be reserved for the demanding subcase of corrective control rather than used as a blanket label for any stable cluster.

## 2. Strengths

- **The comparandum/instrument distinction is preserved, and measurement is kept off the stabilizer ledger.** Section 4's line "Measurement design supplies the audit trail, not a stabilizer" is exactly the separation this seat cares about. The paper refuses to let a coding scheme masquerade as worldly structure, and it explicitly bars collapsing a language-particular category into "a universal language-internal category." That is the right hygiene for typological work.

- **The demotion machinery gives typology an exit procedure, not just an on-ramp.** Section 5's "thin profile" (a label that "cannot generalize beyond the coding sample") and "scope overreach" map cleanly onto the standard worry about comparative labels that sort the sample and predict nothing out of it. Most realism-friendly kind theories tell you when a category is *in*; this one tells you when to throw one *out*. That is genuinely useful to a coder.

- **The level-marking discipline names the confound this seat fights about.** "Wrong-level projection" (a grammatical category in one language doesn't by itself establish a cross-linguistic comparandum) is the descriptive-category-vs-comparative-concept problem stated as a failure mode with a required bridge argument. Charitably, the paper has built the comparative-concept caution into its general apparatus rather than bolting it on.

## 3. Weaknesses (actionable)

- **The typology case gives no operational guidance, and the paper knows what operational guidance looks like because it supplies it elsewhere.** Section 4's truth-tracking paragraph delivers a *contrastive* prediction ("self-consistency should help where coherence is the missing support, while retrieval or tools should help only when they strengthen the needed stabilizer"). The typology paragraph, by contrast, asks only that a comparandum "travel across languages" and "support out-of-sample projection." For an actual dataset that is unworkable as written: how many languages, sampled how, what counts as out-of-sample, what defeats the projection, what threshold triggers demotion to "too thin"? Fix: give the typology case its own contrastive, coder-runnable test, parallel to the truth-tracking one.

- **"Becomes a naturalized kind claim" reopens the door the paper just shut, in a single under-argued sentence.** Section 4 says a comparative concept "can remain an instrument for measurement" but "becomes a naturalized kind claim only if the comparandum shows a profile that travels across languages." If comparative concepts are stipulated instruments for cross-linguistic measurement, the conditions under which one graduates to a *kind* are precisely where the descriptive/comparative confusion creeps back. The whole transition rides on `\citep{reynolds2025comparanda}` (flag: I have not read this source; the load-bearing argument is offloaded to it). Fix: state the graduation conditions in-paper, and say explicitly whether a graduated comparandum is still usable as a neutral measurement instrument or whether naturalization changes its coding role.

- **The typology stabilizer list risks being the "decorative mechanism" the paper warns against.** "The stabilizing background may include acquisition, processing, diachrony, discourse pressure, and literate or institutional transmission" is a grab-bag with no guidance on how a typologist would tell which one is operating or test it. By Section 3's own rule (if removing the mechanism leaves the predictions unchanged, it hasn't earned its place), this list currently fails the test it is meant to license. Fix: pick one comparandum and show one stabilizer doing projective work, with the prediction that goes away if you delete it.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

Take a contested comparative concept — say, your own treatment of "adjective" or "passive" as a comparative concept — and run it through the profile package on a 50-language sample: what is the declared projection, what observation demotes it to "thin," and at what point does it cross from "measurement instrument" to "naturalized kind"? If you can't give me the demotion threshold in coder-operational terms, how is this more than a relabelling of the comparative-concept program?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The framework is conceptually sound and the projectibility payoff is real, but the typology end-user gets a slogan where the truth-tracking case gets a runnable test; make the comparative-concept example operational and disambiguate "naturalized kind claim," and this earns acceptance.

### HPC checklist (as instructed)

- **Is projectibility the epistemic payoff?** Yes, consistently. The paper makes the declared projection the target of every kind claim and orders inquiry around it (Sections 3, 6). No complaint here.
- **Do mechanisms explain projectibility rather than replace it?** Yes, and this is the paper's best move. The Section 3 test ("if removing the mechanism leaves the same predictions in place, the mechanism has not earned its role") is deployed again as a demotion rule (Section 5, "decorative mechanism"). Stabilizers earn their keep by explaining projection, not by existing.
- **Does the conclusion cash out in prediction/explanation/generalization?** Mostly yes (the slogan and the five questions are projection-first), **but not for the typology case specifically** — which is the precise gap this seat is flagging. The deital and truth-tracking examples cash out; the comparative-concept example does not.
