**Reviewer Seat: Matthew H. Slater - Stability Reviewer**

**Source note:** I’m reviewing only the supplied text. I have not independently verified `slater2015` or other cited sources here.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that anti-essentialist kind theory should treat kinds as projectibility profiles: property-and-relation patterns that license specified inferences under scope conditions, with stability, network order, maintenance, and homeostatic control separated as distinct achievements.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper clearly understands the Slater-style point that natural kindness needn’t wait for a named homeostatic mechanism. Section 3 explicitly says Slater’s stability demand is “already projectibility-shaped,” which is exactly the right charitable positioning.

- The distinction among stable profiles, ordered networks, maintainers, and controllers is useful and overdue. It prevents `homeostatic` from becoming an honorific for any recurring cluster.

- Projectibility is not decorative. The abstract, Section 3, demotion rules, and conclusion all make inference the epistemic payoff: the category matters because it lets us predict, explain, or generalize under specified conditions.

3. **Weaknesses**

- Section 3 says “stable property clusters are important but not enough,” but it doesn’t say sharply enough: not enough for what? From the stability seat, this risks making stable-but-non-homeostatic kinds look like preliminary or second-class cases rather than successful kinds with modest but real projectibility.

- The definition of `stabilizer` in Section 2 is too broad. It first seems diachronic or modal, then becomes any relation explaining co-availability rather than mere correlation. That blurs Slater-style stability, Khalidi-style order, and generic evidential support. I’d separate “stable subcluster,” “ordering relation,” and “stabilizing explanation” more cleanly.

- The demotion framework still tilts toward stronger status for categories with identified stabilizers. That’s defensible, but the paper should explicitly protect the evidential standing of stable subclusters where the stability is empirically robust and projectible even though no maintainer or controller is known.

4. **Key Question for Q&A**

When a cluster is stable enough to support reliable local projection but we don’t yet know what, if anything, stabilizes it, does your framework treat that as full natural kindness within a limited scope, or as a merely provisional case awaiting stronger securing claims?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core framework is strong and projectibility is doing real work, but the paper needs to sharpen the status of stable-but-non-homeostatic kinds so stability is treated as an epistemic achievement in its own right, not just a rung below mechanism, maintenance, or control.
