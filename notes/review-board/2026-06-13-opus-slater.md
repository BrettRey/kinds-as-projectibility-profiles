# Review — Matthew H. Slater (Stability Reviewer) seat

*Standpoint seat based on the stable-property-cluster account and the evidential role of stable subclusters. Not a claim about the actual person's views.*

*Source-grounding note: my characterization of the stability-first account (natural kindness without a named homeostatic mechanism; stability as a counterfactually robust modal fact rather than a symptom of an identified maintainer) is from general familiarity with that position, not a re-read of the cited source in this session. Treat it as the seat's standpoint, to be verified against the actual text before it shapes revisions.*

## 1. One-sentence summary

A kind is a "projectibility profile" — a worldly property-and-relation pattern whose partial observation licenses specified inferences under specified conditions — and the Boydian word *homeostatic* should be unbundled into a tiered vocabulary (profile, stabilizer, maintainer, controller) in which corrective control is a demanding subcase rather than the default form of kindhood.

## 2. Strengths

- **It credits stability as already projectibility-shaped, and says so in the right words.** Section 3: "his stability demand is already projectibility-shaped: observing enough of a subcluster should license expectations about the larger cluster." This is the correct reading and not a grudging one. The "over-homeostatization" failure mode (Section 5, fourth) actively *protects* stable, mechanism-unknown clusters from being inflated into homeostasis on the strength of a metaphor. From the stability standpoint, that is the paper's best instinct.
- **Projectibility is genuinely the payoff, and mechanisms explain it rather than replace it.** The decorative-mechanism test (Section 3, reprised as the third demotion rule) is exactly the HPC discipline one wants: "If removing the mechanism leaves the same predictions in place, the mechanism has not earned its role in the kind claim." The conclusion's five questions all cash out in what the category lets you predict, explain, or localize. This paper does not slide from "we named a structure" to "therefore a kind."
- **The epistemic-order / grounding-order distinction (Section 3) defuses the obvious objection.** "We may have good evidence that a profile is projectible before we know what maintains it. The reliable projection still depends on whatever worldly relations make the profile non-accidental." This concedes that you need not *identify* the stabilizer for the kind to be real — the core stability-first commitment — and is the move that keeps the framework honest.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific, actionable)

- **The framework runs "stable" and "stabilized" together, and question 5 presupposes a stabilizer.** Section 2's fifth question asks "what stabilizes that co-availability," and the gloss is "stabilization says why it persists." But on the stability-first view, a cluster's modal robustness can be the ground floor: the honest answer to "what stabilizes it?" is sometimes "nothing further has been identified, and possibly nothing further exists as a separable relation — it is just counterfactually robust." The paper needs to state explicitly that "brute stability, no separable stabilizer" is a *legitimate* profile answer, not an unanswered question or a thin profile. As phrased, the fifth question treats stability as something that must be explained *by* stabilization, which quietly demotes the very cases stability-first theory exists to defend. **Fix:** add one sentence allowing a null/brute answer to Q5, and distinguish stability (a modal property of the profile) from stabilization (a relation that produces it).

- **The paper never says what the stabilizer/maintainer/controller ladder *adds to projectibility* over bare stability — and its own decorative-mechanism test suggests the answer is "often nothing."** Here is the sharp version: if a stable subcluster already licenses the projection (observe part → expect the rest within range), then naming its stabilizer adds no new prediction, and by the Section 3 test the stabilizer "has not earned its role." The paper's own demotion rule thus *elevates bare stability* and threatens to demote stabilizer-talk as decorative. The paper doesn't notice this tension. The defensible reply is available in the text but never made explicit: bare stability licenses *within-range* projection, whereas an identified maintainer/controller licenses *perturbation* and *out-of-range* projection (what the cluster does when you intervene or push it). **Fix:** state the division of projective labour by tier. That both answers "what does stabilization add?" and stops the ladder from looking like a hierarchy of seriousness with stability at the bottom.

- **One sentence does make stable-but-mechanism-unknown kinds second-class, and it contradicts the Section 3 concession.** Section 5, penultimate paragraph: a category "earns stronger status when its profile supports reliable projection, when its stabilizers are identified at the right level, and when its limits are known." The middle clause makes *identifying* a stabilizer a condition on stronger status — which caps a robustly stable, maintainer-unknown kind below an otherwise-equal kind whose mechanism happens to be known. That is precisely the way-station treatment of stability the seat is built to flag, and it sits in direct tension with the epistemic/grounding split the paper earlier endorsed. **Fix:** change the condition to "its stabilizers, *where they exist and have been identified*, are pitched at the right level," so that strength tracks projective reach, not the contingent epistemic luck of having found the mechanism.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

By your own decorative-mechanism test, if a stable subcluster already licenses the relevant projections, then identifying its stabilizer earns nothing and should be demoted as decorative — which makes *stability*, not stabilization, the locus of projectibility. So either (a) the stabilizer/maintainer/controller tiers add only perturbation-and-out-of-range projection, in which case bare stability is a full-status kind for within-range purposes and your "important but not enough" framing (Section 3) is wrong; or (b) they add something to *within-range* projection too, in which case what is it? Which horn do you take?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The framework is HPC-healthy and the projectibility-first discipline is real, but it must separate stability from stabilization, specify what each tier of the ladder adds to projection, and remove the Section 5 clause that makes mechanism-identification a condition on a stable kind's status.
