Taking the “Martin Haspelmath - Typology End-User” seat as a standpoint, not as a claim about his private views.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that anti-essentialist kind claims should be treated as **projectibility profiles**: categories earn realist status by supporting specified inferences under specified conditions, with mechanisms and stabilizers explaining that projectibility rather than replacing it.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper gets the HPC priority right: especially in Sections 2-3 and the conclusion, projectibility is the epistemic payoff, while mechanisms, networks, and stabilizers explain why projection works. This is not a mechanisms-first paper with projectibility added ornamentally.

- Section 2’s separation of diagnostics, profiles, stabilizers, maintainers, and controllers is useful for typology because it prevents a coding instrument from being mistaken for the kind itself.

- Section 5 gives the framework real bite. The failure modes of thin profile, wrong-level projection, decorative mechanism, over-homeostatization, and scope overreach are exactly the kinds of demotion rules typological comparison needs.

3. **Weaknesses**

- Section 4 says a comparative concept becomes a naturalized kind claim only if the comparandum “travels across languages, supports out-of-sample projection, and comes with demotion criteria,” but this remains too programmatic for typological use. The paper should spell out what counts as out-of-sample projection in a typological dataset: unseen languages, unseen constructions, held-out families, diachronic cases, or new diagnostics?

- The paper rightly says “measurement design supplies the audit trail, not a stabilizer,” but the boundary is still underdeveloped. In typology, sampling, coding reliability, feature definitions, and missing-data practices can create the appearance of projection. Section 4 needs sharper rules for distinguishing a genuine cross-linguistic profile from a well-designed comparative coding scheme.

- The demotion rules in Section 5 are strong in principle but not concrete enough for dataset work. A typologist needs decision procedures: when do we localize to a family, relevel to discourse function, split a category, or abandon the comparative concept? The paper should give at least one worked typological example with a failed or demoted category.

4. **Key Q&A Question**

If I hand you a typological feature with partial cross-linguistic clustering, decent coding reliability, and some diachronic plausibility, what exact test would tell me that I have a projectibility profile rather than merely a useful comparative concept?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: the central philosophical distinction is sound and projectibility really does organize the paper, but the typological payoff needs operational procedures before this vocabulary can guide cross-linguistic comparison rather than merely redescribe it.
